# Outline
> Scope of Project

I used to dance pre-professionally in ballet and jazz up until I was about 15. I have since moved to Lion Dancing. However, this project is about my time in this pre-professional setting and how it drastically changed when I left. When I was in middle school, I participated in my first and last dance competition. This dance competition was in a small city, and there were a number of dance studios from around Pennsylvania. I remember seeing quite a scandalous performance that even my mother commented on the appropriateness. After that one competition, my mother and I mutually decided to pull out of the competition program at my dance studio. However, the power this dance had was something I did not expect. Eventually, I pulled out of pre-professional dance due to a number of reasons: finances, mental health, physical demand. At the time, I wasn't sure if it was because I wasn't good enough to keep up with the expectations my dance teacher had on me. Now, I realized it was because the dance studio couldn't keep up with the expectations of what made dance competitions popular. I was too young though to understand.

Fast foward a decade later, I watched a reality TV show about little girls going to dance competitions. I realized the scandalous performance was them, and I watched them perform the very same dance. This show was called "Dance Moms". I began watching more episodes, and I also began watching other dance competitions such as "Dancing with the Stars", "World of Dance", "So You Think You Can Dance", etc. The pattern was clear, the media prioritized wow-factor. This wow-factor meant plugging in stunts, tricks, anything that was considered extremely dangerous in an already dangerous activity.

Then a controversial interview sparked my interest. On February 24, 2026, Timothée Chalamet made a comment claiming that "nobody cares about ballet" anymore. And thus, a project was born.

Do people really not care about ballet anymore? I doubt it, but the interest does hang on the balance. Something also tells me that today's protrayal of dance on film and TV has something to do with it.

I want to discuss the implications of on-screen media has on off-screen dance and dance competitions. It's possible I need to narrow this down to either dance as a whole or just dance competitions since there is quite a bit of context for either or.

As a reader, I want people to understand the implications of how dance is portrayed in media, and how it shapes the viewer's perception of how dance should be off screen. There are a couple questions I hope for people to think about:
- Should dance in media be considered a different style or styles than the traditional styles?
- Is there a way to introduce the younger generation to traditional dance?
- Would they even be interested given the decreasing attention spans as time goes on?
The answer is... I don't know, and I don't intend on answering these questions in my project. I simply want the viewers to reflect on these questions.

However I do want to expose people in how difficult these dance comepetitions really are: financially, physically, and mentally. There is a lot of money that goes into entering a dance competition, and the return on investment isn't always that great - especially if your studio isn't that good.
